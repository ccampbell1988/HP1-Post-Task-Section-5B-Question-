Section B.5 Mental Preparation

Yearly Plan Tools Considerations
Yearly Plan Tools Considerations
1

The Foundation

August - September

Establishing a resilient mindset and introducing core mental skills.

  • Goal Setting: Intro to SMART goals, 'Personal Hockey Vision' worksheets, team workshops.

    Example: A player sets a process goal to "win 70% of puck battles in practice."

    Responsible: Head Coach leads team sessions; Assistants work 1-on-1 with players.

    Tracking: Goal worksheets reviewed monthly in player meetings.

  • Emotional Control: 'Control the Controllables', breathing exercises, 'reset' routines.

    Example: Player uses a "deep breath, stick tap" routine after a mistake.

    Responsible: All coaches reinforce the use of reset routines and cues.

    Tracking: Coach observation; post-practice debriefs on challenging moments.

  • Signal Light System: A mental traffic light (Green=great, Yellow=caution, Red=reset) for quick self-assessment.

    Example: Player feels frustrated (Yellow), uses breathing to get back to Green.

    Responsible: Assistant Coaches introduce and reinforce on the bench.

    Tracking: Players report their "light" status during drill changeovers.

2

Sharpening the Tools

October - December

Refining mental skills in conjunction with on-ice development.

  • Imagery / Visualization: Guided sessions and 'mental reps' before practices.

    Example: Defenseman visualizes a perfect 1-on-1 defensive stop.

    Responsible: Assistant Coach leads sessions; Head Coach adds to pre-practice routine.

    Tracking: Player journals include vividness rating (1-5) of imagery.

  • Highlight Reel Visualization: Players create a mental movie of their best plays to build confidence.

    Example: A forward visualizes their top 3 goals of the season before a game.

    Responsible: Assistant Coach introduces concept; players practice individually.

    Tracking: Players share their "reels" with a line-mate or in their journal.

  • Self-Talk: Workshops on reframing negativity; developing positive cue words.

    Example: Player uses cue "explode" on face-offs or "head up" in traffic.

    Responsible: Head Coach leads workshops; all coaches reinforce usage.

    Tracking: Players log reframed negative thoughts in journals.

  • The Worry Box (Thought Parking): Mentally "park" external worries (school, etc.) until after the game.

    Example: Player "parks" a worry about an exam to focus on the game.

    Responsible: Head Coach introduces concept in a team meeting.

    Tracking: Discussed during 1-on-1 check-ins if a player seems distracted.

3

Performing Under Pressure

January - March

Maintaining toughness, managing fatigue, and peaking for playoffs.

  • Advanced Emotional Control: Mindfulness, high-pressure simulations in practice.

    Example: Practice a 5-on-3 PK for the final 2 minutes of a tied game.

    Responsible: Head Coach designs pressure drills; Mental Performance Consultant may assist.

    Tracking: Success rate in pressure drills; post-game composure self-ratings.

  • Scheduling & Messaging: Consistent pre-game routines and post-game '24-Hour Rule'.

    Example: Post-loss message is brief: "We learn tomorrow. Rest up."

    Responsible: Head Coach owns all team messaging and routine consistency.

    Tracking: Leadership group provides feedback on message clarity and effectiveness.

  • Team Gratitude Circle: A brief ritual to share gratitude, building bonds and perspective.

    Example: "I'm grateful for John blocking that shot for me in the third."

    Responsible: Leadership group or Head Coach facilitates the circle weekly.

    Tracking: Not formally tracked; impact observed in team cohesion.

The Mental Toolkit

Methods for tracking progress and providing feedback.

For the Player

  • Mental Skills Journal: A dedicated notebook for players to log their application of mental skills (e.g., self-talk reframing, imagery vividness) and reflect on their mental state.
  • Goal Setting Worksheets: Structured templates to define and review SMARTER (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound, Evaluated, Rewritten) goals quarterly.
  • Self-Assessment Surveys: Quick digital or paper forms completed post-game to rate confidence, focus, and composure on a 1-5 scale, identifying trends.

For the Coach

  • Athlete Check-Ins: Scheduled 5-minute individual meetings (bi-weekly) to discuss journal entries, goal progress, and any mental roadblocks.
  • Observational Notes: Coaches use a shared digital document or notebook to log specific, observable instances of mental resilience or breakdowns in practices and games.
  • Performance Correlation: Analyzing game stats (e.g., +/- after a mistake, PP%) alongside mental skill focus to highlight the connection between mind and performance.

Coaching Principles

Key considerations for successful implementation.

Recognize that each athlete responds differently to mental skills training. Adapt approaches based on individual personalities and needs, using one-on-one meetings to personalize the program.

This plan is a blueprint, not a rigid script. Be prepared to adjust timelines and focus areas based on team progress, morale, and unforeseen challenges throughout the season.

Maintain open, honest, and consistent communication about the purpose and process of mental training. Create a safe space for athletes to discuss their mental game without judgment.

Foster a supportive, growth-oriented environment where mistakes are viewed as learning opportunities. A positive culture is the foundation upon which mental skills can be effectively built.
